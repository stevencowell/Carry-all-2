Week 1: Safety Refresher & Introduction to Joinery

Welcome to the Tool Carry-all Project

This week sets the foundation for the entire unit. Before we pick up any tools, we’re focusing on two essential things: working safely and understanding the basic joinery principles that will shape the entire project. You'll also begin preparing your timber and learning to mark it accurately — a skill that makes or breaks quality woodworking.

Workshop Safety: Thinking Before You Act

A safe workshop isn’t just about following rules — it’s about mindset. In every action you take, think ahead. Are your safety glasses on? Are you aware of where others are working? Have you kept your workspace clear of offcuts and tripping hazards?

Your most important safety tool is your brain. Machines only do what you tell them, so stay focused, and if you’re tired or distracted, stop and reset. Always wear the required PPE:

  • Safety glasses for eye protection, even if you’re not using machines
  • Closed, leather shoes to protect your feet from dropped tools or timber
  • Hearing protection when using noisy tools
  • Dust masks for sanding and cutting jobs that raise fine particles
  • Hair tied back, no loose clothing, and never wear gloves around spinning machinery

Maintaining a clean workspace is part of safety. Tools left on benches or the floor are a hazard. At the end of every lesson, pack up your bench, sweep your area, and return tools properly.

Foundational Joinery: A Preview

This project is your first introduction to high-quality joinery. While we won’t start cutting joints this week, we’ll introduce two of the corner joints you’ll choose from later:

  • Dovetail joints, known for their angled interlocking shape and mechanical strength
  • Box-pin (finger) joints, known for their large gluing surface and strong, geometric appearance

Understanding these options early helps you visualise how your carry-all will come together and start thinking like a designer.

Marking Out Timber with Accuracy

Before cutting any timber, you must establish your reference surfaces:

  • Plane one face side and one face edge flat and square to each other.
  • Clearly mark them with an "f" for face and a "V" for edge.

Every future measurement depends on these surfaces being true. You’ll begin using tools like:

  • Try square for 90° lines
  • Marking gauge for parallel lines
  • Sharp pencil or marking knife for clean layout lines

Tip: Always mark the waste side of the cut and remember the rule — measure twice, cut once. Cutting on the waste side preserves your measured piece and helps you achieve better fits.

Resource Tip: Dovetail vs Box-Pin Joints

Next week, we’ll dive deeper into the differences between dovetail and box-pin joints. For now, start looking at examples of each in boxes, drawers, and furniture. Ask yourself: Which look do you prefer? Which do you think is easier to make?
