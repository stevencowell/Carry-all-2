Week 4: From Workshop to Factory – How Joinery Scales Up

Introduction

This week we step out of the school workshop and into the world of industrial production. You’ll compare how joints and timber projects are made in class versus how they’re made in factories using advanced tools like CNC routers and laser cutters.

Relative vs Absolute Measurement

In the workshop, we use relative measurement—lining up parts, tracing shapes, and making one piece fit another. Factories rely on absolute measurement, where every part must match an exact size (e.g., 30.00mm) so it fits any other part off the line. No “close enough” allowed within tight tolerances.

Templates and Jigs

To make production faster and more accurate, both schools and factories use:

  • Templates—patterns for tracing consistent shapes
  • Jigs—devices that hold workpieces and guide tools for repeat cuts

CNC Routers and Laser Cutters

CNC routers follow computer instructions to cut timber into perfect shapes repeatedly. Laser cutters use a focused beam of light for precise cuts and fine details. Both depend on accurate digital design files.

Sustainable Joinery: Finger Jointing at Scale

Factories use large-scale finger-jointing to join short offcuts into long boards, minimizing waste. It’s a smart way to recycle material and improve sustainability.
