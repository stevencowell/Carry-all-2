Week 6: Timber, Sustainability & Smart Design

Introduction

This week’s focus is on timber as a sustainable building material — what that means, why it matters, and how design choices affect the environment. Wood isn’t just a beautiful, workable material — it’s also one of the most sustainable if managed correctly. We'll explore how choices around timber species, manufacturing processes, and design can reduce environmental impact.

You’ll also be asked to think critically about your own Carry-all project. Are you using materials efficiently? Could your design waste less wood or last longer?

Why Timber is a Renewable Resource

Unlike concrete, metal, or plastic, timber comes from trees — living organisms that grow back over time. If harvested responsibly (e.g., from plantations or sustainably managed forests), timber is:

  • Renewable – more trees can be planted
  • Biodegradable – it breaks down naturally
  • Carbon storing – trees absorb CO₂ as they grow, and that carbon remains stored in the timber

Because of this, timber products (like your Carry-all) have a much smaller carbon footprint compared to other materials — especially if they’re well-made and long-lasting.

Smart Use of Timber in Industry

Modern timber production is becoming increasingly eco-conscious. Techniques like:

  • Finger-jointing offcuts into longer boards
  • Engineered timbers (like CLT and Glulam)
  • Water-based finishes and glues
  • Optimisation software to plan cuts and reduce waste

Designing for the Environment

Sustainable design starts with asking questions like:

  • How long will this product last?
  • Can it be repaired instead of replaced?
  • Can offcuts be reused?
  • Is the timber sourced ethically?

In your Carry-all, you can apply this thinking by avoiding mistakes that cause re-cutting, reducing waste, and treating your timber with care so it lasts for years.

Discussion Starters

  • How does timber compare to plastic or steel in environmental impact?
  • What are simple ways to reduce waste in student projects?
  • How might your thinking about timber change after knowing its environmental benefits?
